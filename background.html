How we got here.

A few years ago, the City of Gardner brought a roughly 300-acre piece of farmland inside its city limits. The boundary was drawn tightly around that farmland while leaving the surrounding rural homes outside it. The result is procedural — but it is not small. The families closest to the proposed data center have no vote on the City Council decision that will determine what gets built there. The detail below explains how that happened, and what tools remain.

What happened in 2023

On the City of Gardner's books, the parcel where Beale Infrastructure has proposed a hyperscale data center is identified by Ordinance 2739, adopted in 2023. That ordinance brought the ~300-acre agricultural parcel into the corporate limits of the City of Gardner.1

It was not the only annexation in the area. Three further ordinances — Ord. 2769, Ord. 2775, and Ord. 24-16 — annexed nearby agricultural parcels in a similar pattern over the following months.1

Annexation is the legal mechanism by which a city extends its corporate limits over a piece of land. Once land is annexed, the city has zoning authority over it, can apply municipal taxes, can extend utilities, and — critically here — can rezone it from agricultural to industrial use.

Who was left out

The line drawn by the annexation matters because Kansas annexation law generally requires a city to follow contiguous boundaries. The City of Gardner could have included nearby occupied residential parcels at the same time. It did not. The ordinance language brought the agricultural parcel into the city while leaving the residential parcels on three sides of it in unincorporated Johnson County.1

The practical effect is procedural but consequential:

  • The City of Gardner has zoning authority over the parcel and will hold the rezoning hearings and final vote.
  • Residents who live on the immediately adjacent parcels are not Gardner residents, and so are not represented by the City Council that will decide what gets built next door.
  • Their elected representative on land use is the Johnson County Board of Commissioners — but Johnson County has no zoning authority over land that has been annexed into a city.

Subsequent annexations (Ord. 2769, 2775, and 24-16) followed similar patterns: agricultural parcels brought in, residential parcels left out.1

What this means in practice

If you live on a residential parcel adjacent to the proposed site and were not annexed in 2023, your address is in unincorporated Johnson County. You can still attend Gardner Planning Commission and City Council meetings and submit public comment, and that comment becomes part of the record. But you do not vote in Gardner municipal elections, and you do not have a Gardner ward or council member representing you.

What Kansas law says

K.S.A. 12-520 — Annexation procedures

Kansas annexation law (K.S.A. 12-520) sets out the categories of land a city may annex unilaterally and the categories that require either owner consent or a public petition.2 Of particular relevance here:

  • Land of 21 acres or more used for agricultural purposes generally cannot be annexed without the written consent of the owner.2
  • Owners of land may also voluntarily petition the city to be annexed in (a "consent annexation").2

The Ord. 2739 annexation appears to fit the consent-annexation pattern: the owner of the agricultural parcel petitioned to be annexed in. Adjacent residential owners, who did not petition in, were not included.

K.S.A. 12-757 — Protest petitions on rezoning

When a city moves to rezone already-annexed land — which is the relevant step for the Beale Infrastructure proposal — Kansas law (K.S.A. 12-757) provides for a protest petition.3 If the owners of 20% or more of the property within 1,000 feet of the rezoning area file a valid protest petition within the statutory window, the rezoning requires a 3/4 supermajority of the City Council to pass instead of a simple majority.3

Adjacent unincorporated property owners can sign protest petitions on Gardner rezoning actions where their land lies within the 1,000-foot radius. Details on how to organize and verify a protest petition are on the Take Action page.

What residents can do

Several tools remain available to residents in the unincorporated area:

  • Public comment. Anyone — Gardner resident or not — can attend Planning Commission and City Council meetings and submit written comments. These become part of the official record and are reviewed by elected officials.
  • Protest petition under K.S.A. 12-757. If you own property within 1,000 feet of the rezoning area, your signature can count toward the 20% threshold that triggers the supermajority requirement.
  • Engage your County Commissioner. Johnson County's elected officials cannot vote on Gardner zoning, but they represent unincorporated residents and can speak publicly on regional planning, infrastructure, and county-level engagement.
  • Voluntary annexation, in some cases. Some unincorporated property owners may have the option to petition for voluntary annexation into Gardner, which would give them voting rights in city elections going forward. This is a significant decision with tax and service implications and should be discussed with City Hall and, if appropriate, an attorney.
Live in the unincorporated area?

You may have options.

Voluntary annexation, protest petitions under K.S.A. 12-757, and county-level engagement are all tools available to affected residents. The Take Action page has the next steps.

Open questions

Residents have a reasonable interest in understanding why the boundary was drawn the way it was. Among the questions worth asking the City:

  • What was the City's stated rationale for the boundary of Ord. 2739, and the related Ord. 2769, 2775, and 24-16?
  • Were adjacent residential owners offered the option of voluntary annexation at the same time? If not, why not?
  • Was a hyperscale data center contemplated by the City or by any party at the time of the 2023 annexation? Were any pre-annexation discussions or memoranda of understanding signed?
  • What protections, if any, are in place to ensure that adjacent unincorporated residents have a meaningful voice in the rezoning process?

These are not accusatory questions. They are basic process questions, and they deserve clear public answers. As the City responds, this page will be updated.
