Public Notice · Gardner, Kansas

Say no to an industrial-scale data center in Gardner.

Out-of-state developers are quietly buying up Kansas farmland for hyperscale data centers. We're a small town with families, schools, and rural neighborhoods — not a place for a 24/7 industrial campus next door. This is a community information hub: learn the real risks, and help us push back before decisions move forward.

What it actually looks like

This isn't a tech office. It's a heavy industrial campus.

Marketing drawings can make these projects look like sleek, low-profile buildings. The reality is closer to a power plant: a fenced industrial site that runs around the clock, with cooling towers, exhaust stacks, transformer yards, and rows of diesel backup generators.

How it could happen

What "M-1 industrial zoning" actually means

A hyperscale data center is heavy industry. Before one can be built next to homes and schools, the land has to be rezoned to allow industrial use — typically M-1. That single decision opens the door to a lot more than one building.

1

Land is bought up quietly

Developers and their LLCs negotiate with rural landowners — often under non-disclosure agreements — assembling hundreds of acres before the public hears anything official. By the time it's on a public agenda, the deals are already done.

2

The parcel is rezoned to M-1

M-1 is a heavy industrial designation. It permits not just a data center but a wide range of other industrial uses. Once land is rezoned, that classification stays — even if this specific project never gets built or moves on after a few years.

3

Buffer zones get skipped

Properly planned M-1 sites are surrounded by step-down buffers — light industrial, then commercial, then residential. Spot-rezoning a parcel in the middle of a rural area places heavy industry directly next to homes, with no buffer at all.

4

Precedent is set for more

Once one industrial campus is approved in a previously rural area, the next request is harder to deny. Property values, neighborhood character, and long-term land-use plans all shift — for decades.

We're not anti-development. Thoughtful, planned growth is good for Gardner. But heavy industry next to homes, schools, and farms — pushed through quickly, with little public review — isn't growth. It's a permanent change to the character of our community, made on someone else's timeline.

What's actually at stake

The real costs of an industrial-scale data center

These are the facts developers tend to leave out of the glossy presentation. Each one is worth a closer look before any vote.

Water

Cooling a data center takes a lot of water — every single day.

Hyperscale data centers cool themselves with massive evaporative cooling systems. A typical 100 megawatt facility consumes around 530,000 gallons of water per day — roughly equal to the total daily household use of 6,500 homes. Larger AI-focused campuses can use multiples of that.

That water doesn't come from nowhere. It either pulls from the same aquifers and treatment plants our homes and farms rely on, or it requires the city to build entirely new infrastructure — which residents typically end up paying for through rates and bonds.

Source: U.S. data centers used roughly 17.5 billion gallons of water in 2023, and per-facility use is rising as AI workloads grow.

Power & your electric bill

One data center can pull as much power as a small city — and the grid upgrades aren't free.

A single hyperscale campus can require hundreds of megawatts of continuous electrical capacity. Hooking that up to the grid means new substations, new transmission lines, and major upgrades to local infrastructure.

Those upgrade costs are often passed through to ratepayers. In communities across the country, residents have watched their utility bills rise after a data center came in — to pay for capacity they don't use and didn't ask for.

Meanwhile, when the grid is strained, data centers run on diesel backup. That moves the load — and the pollution — straight into your air.

Noise

The fans never turn off. Neither do the generators when they test.

Cooling fans, chillers, and transformers produce a continuous low-frequency hum measurable for hundreds of feet — often in the 65–85 dB range at the property line, comparable to standing next to a busy highway around the clock. That's the steady-state noise.

On top of that, every facility has rows of diesel backup generators that must be tested regularly — typically monthly, often more — and run during any grid disruption. Generator testing is loud enough to rattle windows in nearby homes.

This isn't theoretical. Communities living next to existing data centers — Chandler, AZ; Loudoun County, VA; Prince William County, VA — have all reported sleep disruption and chronic noise complaints.

65–85 decibels
continuous, day and night
Air quality

"Clean tech" runs on dirty diesel.

Every hyperscale data center keeps a large fleet of diesel backup generators on site. Federal rules require those generators to be tested regularly, and they kick in during any grid event. The result is regular emissions of diesel particulate matter (PM2.5), nitrogen oxides, and other air pollutants — directly into a previously rural area.

PM2.5 is small enough to enter the bloodstream through the lungs. It's strongly associated with asthma attacks, reduced lung capacity, cardiovascular disease, and increased emergency-room visits — especially in children, the elderly, and people with existing health conditions.

Schools, ballfields, and homes downwind of the proposed site would see those impacts first.

Tax abatements

The "good for the tax base" argument deserves scrutiny.

Data center developers almost always negotiate large property-tax abatements as a condition of building. In some communities they've ended up paying only a fraction of what comparable industrial property would owe — sometimes for 10, 15, or 20 years.

That means the promised tax revenue is often much smaller than the headlines suggest. Meanwhile, the local government still has to pay for road wear, emergency-services capacity, water and sewer upgrades, and schools.

Before any vote, residents deserve to see the actual proposed tax structure in writing — not just a press release.

Precedent

One M-1 approval changes the future of every neighboring parcel.

Zoning decisions don't reset. Once a parcel of rural land is converted to heavy industrial, the surrounding land becomes a much easier target for the next industrial proposal. The character of the area shifts — and so does what your neighbors can do with their property.

Property values around new industrial sites typically drop. Long-term community plans that families and farmers built around get rewritten by a single zoning vote.

And the company that asked for the rezoning may not be the one that ends up using the land. M-1 is permanent; specific projects are not.

The data center bubble

What happens if the AI build-out slows down?

The current rush to build hyperscale data centers is being driven by an AI infrastructure boom. Industry analysts and major investors are openly warning that this build-out has bubble characteristics: enormous capital expenditure, speculative demand projections, and a narrow set of customers.

If the bubble cools — or if a developer's plans change — Gardner could be left with a partially built or abandoned industrial site, the rezoning still in place, and no easy way back. The risk doesn't sit with the developer. It sits with the community.

Responsible local government plans for the downside, not just the brochure.

Community Impact

Why residents are concerned

These concerns are local and practical. They reflect the questions families are asking about long-term quality of life, neighborhood planning, and a fair public process.

01

Proximity to homes

Industrial operations would sit directly next to established neighborhoods, with no real buffer between heavy equipment and people's backyards.

02

Noise and generators

Cooling systems run continuously and backup generators test regularly. Both can be heard — and felt — in nearby homes.

03

Water and power demand

One facility can use as much water and power as thousands of households. Local infrastructure isn't built for that load.

04

Utility cost concerns

Residents deserve clarity on who pays for the new substations, water lines, and road work — and whether local ratepayers absorb the cost.

05

Rezoning precedent

Approving M-1 here makes the next industrial proposal — by any developer — much harder to stop.

06

Lack of transparency

NDAs, shell LLCs, and rushed timelines have kept residents in the dark. We need the full timeline, the full plan, and an honest impact study.

Fact Check

Myths vs. facts

Developers and their PR teams use the same talking points across the country. Here are the most common ones — and what the evidence actually says.

Myth

"Data centers are quiet."

Fact

Modern facilities can muffle some noise, but cooling fans run 24/7 and emergency generators must be tested regularly. Neighbors of existing data centers consistently report a constant low-frequency hum — and disruptive generator testing.

Myth

"They don't use much water."

Fact

A typical 100 MW data center uses about 530,000 gallons of water per day for evaporative cooling — roughly the same as 6,500 homes. Newer AI-focused designs can use even more during hot weather, exactly when local water systems are already stressed.

Myth

"It's just like any other commercial building."

Fact

It isn't. A hyperscale data center is a heavy industrial facility with industrial-grade power, water, noise, and emissions impacts. That's why it requires M-1 industrial zoning — not standard commercial zoning.

Myth

"It will lower your taxes."

Fact

Data center deals almost always include long-term property-tax abatements — often 10–20 years. The promised revenue rarely matches the headline number, and the city still pays for the new infrastructure the project requires.

Myth

"It will create lots of local jobs."

Fact

A hyperscale data center creates a small number of permanent on-site jobs once built — typically a few dozen for a campus the size of a small city. The construction phase brings temporary jobs, but most are filled by traveling crews, not local hires.

Myth

"This will help the environment because it's modern."

Fact

"Modern" doesn't mean clean. These facilities draw enormous amounts of electricity (often from fossil-fuel plants), use large volumes of water, and run diesel backup generators on site. The environmental impact is real and local.

Children at Special Risk

Children are not small adults

Their lungs, brains, and cardiovascular systems are still developing — which can make industrial emissions and continuous noise more harmful over time.

🫁

Lung development

Diesel PM2.5 from generator testing is associated with asthma, reduced lung capacity, and increased ER visits in children. These impacts can last a lifetime.

🧠

Cognitive impact

Research links air pollution near schools to measurable reductions in cognitive function and lower test scores. Developing brains are uniquely vulnerable.

❤️

Cardiovascular & noise

Industrial pollution can raise blood pressure in children. Continuous 65–85 dB noise is linked to reading delays, attention deficits, and chronic stress in school-age kids.
