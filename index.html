What's actually being proposed

A data center is heavy industry — not a tech office.

Marketing renderings make these projects look like sleek campuses. Built and operated, they are fenced industrial sites that run around the clock, with cooling equipment, transformer yards, and rows of diesel backup generators. Why it matters

The impacts extend far past the parcel boundary.

Each item below is covered in detail on the Concerns page with sourced figures. The short version:

💧

Water

Cooling these facilities takes large volumes of water. "Closed-loop" is a developer commitment, not a regulatory requirement.

⚡

Power

Massive new electrical demand. Grid-upgrade costs are often spread across all ratepayers depending on tariff structure.

🔈

Noise & light

24/7 cooling fans and routine generator testing. Industrial-scale lighting in a previously rural sky.

🏠

Property values

Peer-reviewed research consistently shows lower home values for residential property near large industrial neighbors.

🔥

Emergency response

Diesel storage, lithium-battery backup, and specialty fire-suppression chemicals raise the bar on local response.

📊

Long-term precedent

Industrial zoning is permanent. Approving it here makes the next industrial proposal in the area harder to stop.

How we got here

The land was brought into Gardner. The neighbors weren't.

The parcel was brought into Gardner's city limits a few years ago. The boundary was drawn around the farmland but not around the homes on three sides of it.

The practical result: families who live directly next to the proposed data center are not Gardner residents and have no vote on the City Council decision that will determine what gets built next door.
