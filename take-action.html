A weeknight evening

Attend the May 13 and May 15 public meetings.

These are the City's formal engagement opportunities before the Planning Commission hearing. They are the right time to ask questions, listen to other residents, and let the City know how many people are paying attention.

  • May 13, 2026 · 6:00 p.m. — Public Meeting #1. City Hall, 120 E. Main St., Gardner.
  • May 15, 2026 · 6:00 p.m. — Public Meeting #2. Same location.

Verify the time and location with the City Clerk before traveling. The full timeline (including the Planning Commission hearing and Council vote) is on the Timeline page.

A weeknight evening

Speak at the May 26 Planning Commission hearing.

This is where the Planning Commission makes its formal recommendation to the City Council. Public comment is taken, and your remarks become part of the official record. This is the meeting where in-person testimony has the most direct impact.

What to do:

  • Arrive early. Sign up for public comment at the meeting (sign-up sheets are typically by the door).
  • Plan for a 2–3 minute statement. Practice it once at home.
  • Lead with who you are and where you live. Then make one or two clear points. Cite numbers if you have them.
  • End with a specific ask: "I'm asking the Commission to recommend denial of the rezoning."
Sample 3-minute structure
  • Open (15s): "My name is [Name]. I live at [Street, City]." If unincorporated, say so plainly.
  • Why this matters to you (60s): One personal sentence about your home, your kids, your farm, your well water — whatever is real for you.
  • One or two specifics (90s): Choose one or two impacts (water, noise, traffic, generators, property values, precedent) and cite a real number.
  • Ask (15s): "I'm asking the Commission to recommend denial of the rezoning. Thank you."
15 minutes

File a written comment, even if you can't attend.

Written comments submitted to the Planning Commission and City Council before the hearing become part of the official record and are reviewed by elected officials. Written comment is meaningful even if you cannot attend in person.

  • Email the City Clerk: cityclerk@gardnerkansas.gov with a clear subject line referencing the rezoning.
  • Email the Mayor and Council directly. The Representatives page has a one-click "email all five Council members at once" link and a ready template.
  • Keep it short and personal. A few honest sentences from a real resident outweigh a long generic letter.

A few weeks · property owners only

Consider a protest petition under K.S.A. 12-757.

Kansas law gives nearby property owners a formal tool to raise the bar on rezoning. Under K.S.A. 12-757, owners of 20% or more of the property within 1,000 feet of the rezoning area can sign a valid protest petition. If a valid petition is submitted within the statutory window, the rezoning requires a 3/4 supermajority of the City Council to pass instead of a simple majority.1

The mechanics:

  • Who can sign? Owners (not renters) of property within 1,000 feet of the rezoning area. Both Gardner residents and adjacent unincorporated Johnson County property owners can sign if their land is within the radius.
  • What does it count? The 20% threshold is measured by total property area within 1,000 feet — not by number of signers. A handful of larger parcels can quickly add up.
  • Deadline. Filed in writing with the City Clerk, generally within 14 days after the Planning Commission hearing. The protest petition deadline is approximately June 9, 2026 — verify the exact deadline with the City Clerk.
  • Form. Each signature must be the owner of record, with the legal description of their property. Most successful protest petitions are organized with a notary on hand and supported by an attorney.
If you own land within 1,000 feet of the parcel

Your signature on a valid protest petition can directly raise the threshold for approval. The first practical step is to get a list of the affected parcels and reach out to those owners. A subsequent Protest Petition page on this site will publish a sample petition form and step-by-step organizing guide. In the meantime, talk to your neighbors and consider engaging an attorney experienced in Kansas land-use law.

5 minutes · unincorporated residents

Contact your Johnson County Commissioner.

If you live in unincorporated Johnson County (which includes most of the residential properties immediately adjacent to the proposed site), your direct elected representative on county matters is the Johnson County Board of Commissioners. The Board does not vote on Gardner zoning decisions, but County Commissioners can speak publicly on regional planning, infrastructure capacity, and the impact on unincorporated residents.

The 6th District (which covers much of southern Johnson County including the Gardner/Edgerton area) is represented by Commissioner Shirley Allenbrand.

  • County website: jocogov.org/department/board-county-commissioners
  • Verify her current office contact information through the county site before sending.
