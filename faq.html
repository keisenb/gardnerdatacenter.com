Myths & facts.

Developers and their PR teams use the same talking points across the country. Here are the most common ones, with what the evidence actually says.

Myth

"Data centers bring lots of jobs."

Fact

Construction creates temporary jobs, mostly filled by traveling crews. Once operational, hyperscale campuses are highly automated. A 300-acre campus typically employs only a few dozen permanent staff. Published staffing figures from comparable hyperscale facilities support that range. The on-site headcount is small compared with the size of the buildings.

Myth

"Property tax revenue from the data center will lower everyone else's taxes."

Fact

Property-tax abatements are common for data centers. Many states and cities offer abatements or PILOT (payment-in-lieu-of-tax) agreements that significantly reduce or eliminate local property tax revenue from the facility for 10 to 30 years — exactly the period when local impacts (noise, traffic, infrastructure stress) are greatest. Residents should ask whether any abatement, PILOT, or industrial revenue bond is being negotiated, and what the net revenue picture actually looks like across the abatement window.

Myth

"Closed-loop cooling means almost no water use."

Fact

Closed-loop systems do use less water than evaporative cooling, but they still require periodic makeup water, plus water for site needs (potable, fire, irrigation, dust control). More importantly, cooling design is generally not codified in local ordinance — it lives in the developer's design documents. Operators can convert from closed-loop to higher-water-use cooling later for capacity, performance, or workload reasons. Residents deserve enforceable, written commitments — with measurable performance standards and inspection authority — not just marketing language.

Myth

"The grid upgrades won't cost ratepayers anything."

Fact

That depends entirely on the tariff structure approved by the Kansas Corporation Commission. Under many existing large-load tariffs, the cost of new transmission and substation capacity is partially socialized across the broader ratepayer base. The specific tariff terms — not the developer's reassurance — determine who pays. The relevant filings are public and worth scrutiny. See the Concerns page.

Myth

"Data centers are quiet."

Fact

Modern facilities can muffle some noise, but cooling fans run 24/7 and emergency generators must be tested regularly. Field measurements at operating campuses commonly show continuous property-line noise in the 60–85 dBA range. Communities living next to existing data center clusters — including in Loudoun County, VA and Chandler, AZ — have reported sleep disruption and chronic noise complaints.

Myth

"If it doesn't go here, it'll just go somewhere else."

Fact

Hyperscale siting is highly constrained by power, water, fiber, and land. Communities that have organized and imposed meaningful conditions — buffer setbacks, noise standards, water-supply caps, decommissioning bonds, real tax-abatement scrutiny — have repeatedly modified or blocked projects. Developers move to communities better suited to absorb the impact. The fact that a project could go somewhere else is not a reason to accept it here on whatever terms are offered.

Myth

"Industrial zoning is normal for areas near logistics parks."

Fact

The presence of nearby industrial development does not automatically make additional industrial development appropriate, especially at this scale. Hyperscale data center impacts (continuous noise, light, water, traffic, electrical infrastructure) extend far beyond the parcel boundary. M-1 industrial zoning adjacent to existing rural residential land is a significant change in land-use character — and it shapes what gets built on every adjacent parcel for decades to come.

Myth

"It's just like any other commercial building."

Fact

It isn't. A hyperscale data center is a heavy industrial facility with industrial-grade power, water, noise, and emissions impacts. That's why it requires M-1 industrial zoning — not standard commercial zoning. The zoning category itself is the clearest signal that this is not a normal commercial development.

Myth

"This will help the environment because it's modern."

Fact

"Modern" doesn't mean clean. These facilities draw enormous amounts of electricity (often from fossil-fuel-heavy grids), use real volumes of water, and run diesel backup generators on site. The local environmental impact — air, noise, light, water, traffic — is real, regardless of what corporate sustainability reports say.

Myth

"You're just a small group of NIMBYs."

Fact

The questions on this site — about water, power, noise, air quality, taxes, decommissioning, emergency response, and process — are the same questions raised by communities of every size and political stripe across the country. They are reasonable land-use questions for any heavy industrial proposal next to homes, schools, and farms. Asking them is not obstructionism; it is what a healthy planning process is supposed to do.
